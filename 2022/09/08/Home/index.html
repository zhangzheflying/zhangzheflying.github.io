Overall scientific approach

The overall approach to solve the objectives integrates valuable data from 13 long-term experimental sites in Southern China with innovative modelling, including:

1) using data collection about agricultural management, climate, and soil properties to assess the impact of site conditions on the NUE.

2) assessing the BC balance to derive site specific acidification rates (AR) in relation to site conditions.

3) assessing the role of N surplus, BC surplus and CEC (pH=7 ) for soil pH change in non-calcium soil.

4) using a subset of the 13 long-term experiments to calibrate VSD+ and use it for evaluation of multiple fertilizer strategies (if possible, with lime).

BASIC THEORY:

Phosphorus has a high affinity for soil particles, causing low availability to plants and low recovery fertilizer efficiency. Soil P exists in various forms, including dissolved, weakly and strongly bonded and stable P. Dissolved P is directly available for crop growth. Strongly and weakly boned P can resupply dissolved P and contribute to crop growth. Adsorption and desorption are the main processes contributing to the resupply, and pH, soil organic matter, clay minerals and Fe/Alox are responsible for regulating these processes. Phosphorus is mainly sorbed onto the surfaces of Fe/Alox and clay minerals, which are affected by pH, and anions that compete for ligand-exchange reactions with these surfaces. Over time P can diffuse into these minerals, becoming occluded and thus less available to plants. It may also react with Fe, Al (acid pH) and calcium (Ca) (alkaline pH) ions in solution to form phosphate minerals with varying levels of solubility.

OBJECTIVES AND RESEARCH CONRENT:

This project aims to unravel the factors controlling the potential of soils to supply P, to assess agronomic and environmental benefits of a P index that reflects soil P buffer capacity, and to evaluate the potential of this index for sustainable P management at local and regional scale.
