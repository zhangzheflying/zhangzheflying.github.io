Research objectives 3

Impacts of different management practices and site conditions on soil acidification rates in long-term experiments

Overview of the linkage between acid (H+) production by (i) N deposition (ii) N inputs by fertilizers and (iii) BC uptake and H+ consumption by exchange of H + against BC in soil

Overview of acid (H+) production by C, S and P inputs from deposition and fertilizers

Soil acidification is a dynamic process of H+ production and consumption. H+ is produced in many of the nutrient cycles, including C, N, S, P, etc.. In general, the three main sources of proton (H+) production, include (i) N transformations (ii) plant uptake of cations over anions induced by crop harvesting and (ii) net HCO3- leaching. The effect of acid production is mainly a decrease in the ANC, mainly in terms of a decrease in base cations, since H+ is consumed by different buffer system, including the dissolution of carbonate (in calcareous soils only), weathering of silicate minerals, exchange of H+ with adsorbed BC on clay and organic matter and dissolution of Al-hydroxyl complexes, under different soil pH range. However, adsorption of acid anions such as sulphate also neutralizes the H+ production and causes a decrease in ANC. Next, an assessment will be made of the impact of management (crop type, fertilizer rate, fertilizer type), climate (precipitation, temperature, and sunshine hours) and soil properties (e.g., soil type, SOM, TN, AN, TP, AP) on soil acidification rates by using multi variate (non-linear) regression or machine learning. This allows us to underpin the contribution of different drivers of soil acidification for different site conditions in Southern China.
